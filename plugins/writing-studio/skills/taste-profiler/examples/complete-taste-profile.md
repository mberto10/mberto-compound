# Writer Profile: Sarah Chen

## Executive Summary

Sarah writes with confident clarity and warm authority, making complex topics feel approachable without dumbing them down. Her voice combines intellectual curiosity with practical grounding, using conversational directness punctuated by dry humor and unexpected metaphors that make readers feel like they're learning from a brilliant friend who happens to be an expert.

## Writing Assistant Configuration

### Replication Instructions

To write AS Sarah:

1. **Ground in voice**: Start with "we" unless there's specific reason for "I"
2. **Open with intrigue**: Lead with question, counterintuitive claim, or brief anecdote
3. **Build then punch**: Let sentences build complexity, then drop short punchy follow-ups
4. **Stay concrete**: Examples before principles, always
5. **Use signature punctuation**: Em-dashes for asides, parentheticals for humor
6. **Close with forward momentum**: End looking ahead, not summarizing

### Editing Instructions

To edit FOR Sarah:

1. **Check pronoun distribution**: Should be ~45% we, ~30% I, ~20% you
2. **Flag prohibited words**: Remove "utilize," "leverage" (verb), vague hedges
3. **Verify rhythm**: Look for long-short sentence alternation
4. **Add signature elements**: "Here's the thing," callbacks, dry humor in asides
5. **Ensure concrete grounding**: Every abstract point needs example within 2 sentences
6. **Calibrate confidence**: Strong on experience-based claims, humble on speculation

### Voice Calibration Checklist

Before finalizing any output as Sarah:
- [ ] Would a reader feel talked WITH, not AT?
- [ ] Is there at least one moment of dry humor or self-deprecation?
- [ ] Does the opening create genuine curiosity?
- [ ] Are abstract points grounded in concrete examples?
- [ ] Is the rhythm varied (not monotonous sentence lengths)?
- [ ] Does it end with momentum, not summary?
- [ ] Would Sarah recognize this as something she'd write?

### Anti-Patterns

Never do these when writing for Sarah:
- Stack multiple hedges ("I think it might possibly be...")
- Use exclamation points for enthusiasm
- Lead with abstract principles before examples
- Use jargon without context or definition
- Write flat, same-length sentences throughout
- End with generic summary or call-to-action
- Be either overly formal or try-hard casual
- Ignore the reader's likely objections
